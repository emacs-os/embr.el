embr benchmark: screencast vs screenshot -- 2026-03-20

== Input Responsiveness (screencast wins) ==
Input-to-frame p95:   31.5ms vs 46.0ms  (-31.5%)  << exceeds 20% target
Frame interval p95:   48.4ms vs 55.0ms  (-12.1%)
Effective FPS:        31.7 vs 29.9
FPS 30+ bucket:       76.3% vs 48.1%
Cmd latency max:      2.1s vs 4.3s

== Frame Freshness (screenshot wins) ==
Staleness p95:        23.6ms vs 16.3ms  (+44.2% regression)
Freezes:              3 vs 2

== Ack Health ==
Ack errors:           0
Ack ok:               3349

== Drop Rates ==
Screencast:           37.9% (throttle drops, daemon skips intermediate frames)
Screenshot:           10.3% (input-priority drops only)

== What This Means ==

Screenshot bottleneck was the CDP pipe. Every frame required a
round-trip page.screenshot() RPC that blocked the pipe. Input commands
(click, key, scroll) had to wait for the screenshot to finish before
they could get through. That is why input-to-frame was slow (46ms p95)
and why command ack latency could spike to 4.3s.

Screencast eliminates that bottleneck. The browser pushes frames on
its own, no RPC needed. Input commands flow freely on the CDP pipe
with no contention. Input-to-frame drops to 31.5ms p95. Command ack
max drops by half.

The bottleneck has moved. Now it is on the Emacs render side. The
browser pushes frames faster than Emacs can consume them (37.9% of
frames are throttle-dropped by the daemon to avoid flooding the
pipe). The frames that do reach Emacs are slightly older by the time
they render (staleness p95 went from 16.3ms to 23.6ms) because the
daemon buffers the latest frame and only flushes on a timer.

Next optimization target: Emacs-side render latency (PLAN-5 canvas
acceleration) to close the staleness gap.

== Decision ==
Screencast is the better transport. The input responsiveness gain is
large and directly felt by the user. The staleness regression is
small in absolute terms (7ms at p95) and not perceptible. Recommend
auto as default.
